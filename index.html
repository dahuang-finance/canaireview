Can AI Replace the Peer Reviewer?

Avner Kalay  ·  James Schallheim  ·  Da Huang

AI is being deployed at speed to review scientific work for conferences, journals, and grant panels. But can AI review? We use submissions to the Utah Winter Finance Conference (UWFC), one of the most selective and longest running boutique finance conferences, to find out, and we repeat the study for each new frontier flagship release. Methodology in the companion paper (coming soon).

Findings

Updated on May 3, 2026

  1. AI struggles to identify exceptional papers. The frontier models concentrate roughly three quarters of their scores in the middle two cells of the 1 to 5 scale, while human reviewers use the full range. Prompting AI to be more critical does not solve the problem, since this just shifts the whole distribution toward the lower end without adding discrimination at the top.
  2. AI's rankings track human rankings only weakly. The best rank-order agreement we observe, from Opus 4.7, is 0.31, on a scale where 0 means a random ranking and 1 means complete agreement.
  3. The gap is closing fast. Across three model generations from each vendor, released between November 2025 and April 2026, distributional distance to the human reference (how far AI's score distribution sits from the human one) fell by roughly half, from around 0.85 to 0.45, and rank-order agreement roughly doubled, from around 0.12 to 0.25. This holds for both the Opus 4.5 / 4.6 / 4.7 lineage and the GPT‑5.1 / 5.4 / 5.5 lineage.
