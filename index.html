Can AI Replace the Peer Reviewer?

Avner Kalay  ·  James Schallheim  ·  Da Huang

AI is being deployed at speed to review scientific work for conferences, journals, and grant panels. But can AI review? We use submissions to the Utah Winter Finance Conference (UWFC), one of the most selective and longest running boutique finance conferences, to find out. Each UWFC paper is scored by two members of the program committee on a 1 (best) to 5 (worst) scale. The committee is a rotating pool of 121 leading finance researchers from 2016 to 2025.† AI is asked to score each paper the same way, on the same scale. We repeat the study for every new frontier flagship release from OpenAI and Anthropic since November 2025 (and we plan to do the same for future releases). Detailed methodology in the companion paper (coming soon).

† All review data used in the analysis is anonymized — only the 1–5 score, with no reviewer identity or written comment.

Findings

Updated on May 11, 2026

  1. AI doesn't use the extremes of the scale. Humans use the full 1–5 range and give the top score (1) to 12% of papers. The best frontier AI models pile 74–80% of their scores at 2 or 3 and give a top score to fewer than 4% of papers. Prompting AI to be more or less critical does not solve this — it just shifts the whole distribution without singling out exceptional papers.
  2. Counterintuitively, frontier AI agrees with a single human reviewer about as well as two humans agree with each other. Opus 4.7's score correlates with a randomly chosen human reviewer at 0.24. Two randomly chosen human reviewers correlate with each other at 0.18. So the best AI today is at or modestly above human-reviewer level on per-paper agreement — but this is a recent development, with the previous-generation GPT‑5.1 still sitting at 0.10. Agreement is also a narrow test on this task: only about 18% of any reviewer's rating is shared signal across reviewers, and the remaining 82% is the reviewer's own idiosyncratic signal — taste, priors, what they happened to find compelling. So the more revealing question is not who agrees with whom, but whose evaluations actually track the papers' future impact.
  3. Humans correctly identify the top and bottom papers; AI's edge is in the middle. Using future citations as a quality proxy, humans clearly beat AI at flagging both exceptional papers and clear weak papers. AI's predictive advantage is concentrated in the middle of the distribution, where human reviewers tend to give middling scores. But the calls that determine the conference program are at the top — which papers to accept — and at the bottom — which to clearly reject. Humans flag those papers correctly; AI does not.
  4. The gap is closing fast. From November 2025 to April 2026, OpenAI released GPT‑5.1 → 5.4 → 5.5 and Anthropic released Opus 4.5 → 4.6 → 4.7. Across the two lineages, the distributional distance to the human reference (the L1 distance between the two score histograms) fell by an average of 47%, and the correlation rose by an average of 55%.
