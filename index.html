A living research project

Can AI Replace the Peer Reviewer?

Avner Kalay  ·  James Schallheim  ·  Da Huang

For every new generation of frontier large language models, we send the latest annual program of submissions to the Utah Winter Finance Conference (UWFC) — widely regarded as one of the most selective invitation-only finance research conferences — through the latest reasoning models from Anthropic and OpenAI under a fixed prompt and the same 1–5 scoring scale used by the human program committee. Each paper is independently scored by two senior committee members; we compare. See details of the methodology in the companion paper (coming soon).

Findings log

May 3, 2026

Anthropic Opus 4.5 / 4.6 / 4.7 and OpenAI GPT‑5.1 / 5.4 / 5.5 (released between November 2025 and April 2026)

  1. AI faces a stark tradeoff. It is extremely reluctant to assign low scores — a positivity bias — and at the same time it fails to identify the exceptional papers a human program committee would single out. Humans use the full 1–5 range; the frontier model concentrates roughly three quarters of its scores in the middle two cells of the scale.
  2. AI's rankings track human rankings only weakly. Even on the papers AI does score, the per-paper correlation between the AI score and the mean human score reaches only 0.31 at the frontier, and rank-order agreement is similarly low.
  3. The gap is closing fast. Across three model generations from each vendor, both the distributional distance to the human reference and per-paper rank-order agreement improved monotonically with every release. The newest models close roughly half the gap of the oldest.
