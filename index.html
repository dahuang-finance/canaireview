Can AI Replace the Peer Reviewer?

Avner Kalay  ·  James Schallheim  ·  Da Huang

AI is being deployed at speed to review scientific work for conferences, journals, and grant panels. But can AI review? We use submissions to the Utah Winter Finance Conference (UWFC), one of the most selective and longest running boutique finance conferences, to find out. Each UWFC paper is scored by two members of the program committee on a 1 (best) to 5 (worst) scale. The committee is a rotating pool of 121 leading finance researchers from 2016 to 2025.† AI is asked to score each paper the same way, on the same scale. We repeat the study for every new frontier flagship release from OpenAI and Anthropic since November 2025 (and we plan to do the same for future releases). Detailed methodology in the companion paper (coming soon).

† All review data used in the analysis is anonymized — only the 1–5 score, with no reviewer identity or written comment.

Findings

Updated on May 11, 2026

  1. AI doesn't identify exceptional or clearly weak papers. → Figure 1

    Humans use the full 1–5 range, giving the top score (1) to 12% of papers and the bottom score (5) to 7%. The best frontier AI models give the top score to fewer than 4% of papers, the bottom score to fewer than 2%, and pile 74–80% of their scores at 2 or 3. Prompting AI to be more or less critical does not fix the compression problem — it just shifts the whole distribution without singling out exceptional or weak papers.

  2. Frontier AI's per-paper agreement with human reviewers is weak — but matches, or modestly exceeds, the agreement between two humans. → Figure 2

    Opus 4.7's score correlates with a randomly chosen human reviewer at 0.26. Two randomly chosen human reviewers correlate with each other at 0.19, comparable to the referee-pair correlations Welch (2014) reports for the Journal of Finance (0.15), the Review of Financial Studies (0.14), and the SFS Cavalcade conference (0.20). So the best AI today is at or modestly above human-reviewer level on per-paper agreement — but this parity is a recent development, with the previous-generation GPT‑5.1 still sitting at 0.11. However, disagreement does not necessarily mean poor review quality. The human-to-human correlation of 0.19 translates to reviewers placing 0.33 weight on a shared signal — comparable to JF (0.32), RFS (0.31), and Cavalcade (0.36) in Welch (2014) — and 0.67 weight on their own idiosyncratic signal, such as taste, priors, or what they happen to find compelling. Idiosyncratic does not mean wrong. The more important question is not who agrees with whom, but whose evaluations predict the papers' future impact.

  3. Where humans commit to a strong call — top or bottom — they predict future citations better than AI. In the middle, AI does better. → Figure 3

    Among papers two reviewers rated near the top (mean ≤ 1.5) or near the bottom (mean ≥ 4.0), human scores predict future citations more strongly than AI scores do. The top and bottom are also the two calls that determine the conference program — which papers to put on and which to clearly reject — and human reviewers concentrate their evaluative attention accordingly. In the middle, where reviewers settle on a 2 or 3, the precise score has little bearing on the program outcome, and human scores carry essentially no signal for future citations. AI, which scores every paper with uniform attention, retains its predictive signal in the middle. The conference program is decided where humans commit, so AI's predictive advantage in the middle does not change editorial outcomes.

  4. AI is improving fast. → Figures 4–6

    From November 2025 to April 2026, OpenAI released GPT‑5.1 → 5.4 → 5.5 and Anthropic released Opus 4.5 → 4.6 → 4.7. Over those five months, the distributional distance to the human reference (the L1 distance between the two score histograms) fell by an average of 47% across the two lineages. Per-paper correlation with a single human reviewer rose from 0.11 (GPT‑5.1) and 0.18 (Opus 4.5) to 0.18 (GPT‑5.5) and 0.26 (Opus 4.7). The frontier now matches or modestly exceeds the inter-human benchmark of 0.19. No saturation yet.
